Conception

Lesson One: Writing Styles

Prose

There are many ways to classify writing. For prose (fiction) writing, one method is to use word length. Here are some of the more common categories.

Flash Fiction: 1,000 words or less. Great for writing warm-ups and can easily be accomplished in one sitting. Short Stories: 1,000-7,500 words. These often appear in magazines. Many professional writers started their careers with short stories. Novelette: 7,500-20,000 words. Can be difficult to sell to a print publisher, but great for publishing online. Novella: 20,000-50,000 words. A popular length for the e-publishing market. Novel: 50,000-110,000 words. Young Adult (YA) novels tend to be on the shorter range of the spectrum, but not always. Epic: Over 110,000 words. This generally only works for people who have contracts with traditional publishers.

Another classification is by genre, a category of artisitc composition characterized by similarities in form, style, or subject matter. Many stories incorporate several genres. Below is a listing of a few of the more common forms. Click on a picture to explore more.

Fantasy

Magic, mythology, kingdoms, and quests, often in historical settings. World building is critical.
Popular Subgenres: High/Epic fantasy, low fantasy, urban, contemporary, comic, slipstream, horror, historical, magical realism, sword and sorcery, dark fantasy, fables, fairy tales, and superheroes.

Science Fiction

Stories set in the future or past, often featuring advanced technological concepts. World building is crucial.
Popular Subgenres: Space opera, alternate history, cyberpunk, steampunk, biopunk, military, hard science fiction, soft science fiction, dystopian, and utopian.

Romance

Romantic relationships between people. A lot of sensual tension, desire, and idealism is frequently present.
Popular Subgenres: Contemporary, historical, western, paranormal, futuristic, time travel, gothic, regency, young adult, and romantic suspense.

Adventure

Daring escapes, thrilling feats, and lots of near-misses. High stakes, fast paced, with tension that explodes in the climax.
Popular Subgenres: Action, action-comedy, disaster, epic, escapes, heist, historical, martial arts, paranormal, revenge, romantic, survival, treasure hunts, and westerns.

Mystery

Clues lead to rising tension as the solution approaches. Often intentionally misleading, with a big twist at the end.
Popular Subgenres: Capers, detectives, Noir, historical, cozy mystery, police procedural, hobby mystery, domestic, true crime, and paranormal mystery.

Horror

Stories designed to scare ... only for the brave (or the fearless). You'd better not read these by yourself at night with the lights off...
Popular Subgenres: psychological, slasher, gore, body, gothic, found footage, monster, splatterpunk, paranormal, horror drama, weird fiction, and comedy.

Poetry

When most people think of poetry, they probably imagine something like this:

Roses are red
Yoda is green
Everything is proceeding as I have forseen

Or some other such sillyness. Poetry takes many forms. Like with prose, poetry has many different lengths and styles.

  • Blank verse: Written with a precise meter
  • Rhymed poetry: Rhyme with varied scheme
  • Free verse: Lacks consistent rhyme scheme, metrical pattern or musical form.
  • Haiku: 3 lines: 5 syllables, 7 syllables, 5 again
  • Sonnet: 14 lines and typically about love
  • Ode: Tribute to a subject
  • Limerick: 5 lines; AABBA rhyme scheme
  • Narrative Poetry: Tells a story
  • Ballad: Form of narrative verse that's either poetic or musical. (Think Bob Dylan and The Beatles)

Nonfiction

Expository

This style seeks to inform and educate readers without expressing opinions. Facts, statistics, and data are going to be necessary, as well as citations. This is not a place to try to influence the reader. Examples of expository nonfiction include how-to books, textbooks, cookbooks, and instruction manuals.

Descriptive

The goal with this writing style is to engage the audience's senses and feelings. The reader should feel like they are there with you. This style is not used to narrate. Some examples include novels, poetry, and music lyrics.

Narrative

This style seeks to offer insight into the thoughts and feelings of characters. Narrative writing can be used very effectively in problem-solving nonficton. It is not used to introduce facts nor to persuade an audience. Some examples include poetry, short stories, novels, and screenplays.

Persuasive

This goal of this style is to convince the reader to take action and implement your solution. You'll need to appeal to them on an emotional level, showing your credibility throughout. This is not used for anything outside reality. Common examples include academic papers, opinion pieces, and editorials.
